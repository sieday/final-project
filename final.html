About Me

Hello!

My name is Sienna Day and I am a student focusing on the intersection between geographic information systems and computer science. Over my last three years as an undergraduate at Portland State, I have delved into computer graphics, databases, security, operating systems and more to get a taste of the spectrum of knowledge that computer science offers. This has given me the background to not just use GIS software, but understand the inner workings of tools such as PostGIS, ArcPro, and QGIS to use them to their best ability and deliver a more algorithmically intensive analysis of spatial data.

Throughout university, I have also continued to expand my career in public health and community engagement. With Outside In, I have been a Youth Outreach Intern in charge of connecting with students and parents of a variety of different cultural backgrounds to deliver complex healthcare and program information. This outreach allowed me to stay grounded with the end user and communicate difficult healthcare information in an equitable and accessible way which transfers directly into house I think about both software and mapping. Both of these products are irrelevant if there is not an end user that can effectively use and interpret them. Currently, I work as a Site Coordinator for a pediatric clinic to optimize provider workflows, support clinical staff, and, overall, provide necessary and high impact care for historically underserved youth and their families.

It is my ultimate goal to combine all of these passions. Public health, computer science, accessible communication, and geographic information systems are themes that will continue to drive me. I know that effective products and effective teamwork comes from a multidisciplinary mindset, which I will continue to bring to the table.

    One of the first group projects that I worked on was for my CS300: Intro to Software Development class. This class focused on a ten week term project with multiple team members using a sequential development model. This included documenting requirements, design, and testing along with presenting a final product, This was unlike previous projects where I acted independently and on a much shorter timeline. The main intent of the project was to create a data management system for a fictional medical agency, Chocaholics Anonymous. The practice of documenting functional and nonfunctional requirements required me to think deeper about the intentions of our final product in a way that other projects did not. The final product included a basic user interface with inputs and an updating data structure behind it. Teamwork presented unexpected issues with everyone coming from a different level of knowledge and with different schedules, but with this project we got to overcome these logistical obstacles to create a functional product.
